# The 4% Rule: How Much Can You Safely Withdraw?

Once you retire, the biggest challenge shifts from "how do I save?" to "how do I spend without running out of money?" The 4% Rule is a classic guideline used to answer this question.

The rule states that if you withdraw 4% of your total retirement savings in the first year of retirement, and then adjust that amount for inflation each following year, your money has a very high probability of lasting at least 30 years. For example, if you have $1 million saved, you would withdraw $40,000 in year one. If inflation is 3% the next year, you'd withdraw $41,200, and so on.

The 4% Rule was developed based on historical market performance, assuming a balanced portfolio of 50% stocks and 50% bonds. It is designed to survive even the worst market conditions.

However, it's important to treat this as a rule of thumb rather than a law. Life is unpredictable. If the market has several bad years right after you retire (a "sequence of returns" risk), you might need to reduce your spending. If you retire very early, a 4% withdrawal rate might be too aggressive. Conversely, if you have a very short time horizon, you could likely spend more. Use the 4% rule as a starting point for your planning, but be prepared to be flexible as market conditions change.
