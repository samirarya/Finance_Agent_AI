# The Greeks Explained: Measuring Option Risks

In the world of options trading, "the Greeks" are a set of mathematical measures used to estimate how different factors affect an option's price. For beginners, understanding these four primary Greeks—Delta, Gamma, Theta, and Vega—is essential for managing risk and predicting how a trade might perform.

**Delta** measures how much an option's price is expected to change for every $1 move in the underlying stock. For example, if a call option has a Delta of 0.50, its price should increase by roughly $0.50 for every $1 the stock goes up. Delta is also often used as a rough estimate of the probability that an option will finish "in-the-money."

**Gamma** measures the rate of change in Delta. Think of Gamma as the "acceleration" of an option's price. It tells you how much the Delta will change as the stock price moves. High Gamma means the Delta can change rapidly, which is common for options that are near their strike price and close to expiration.

**Theta** represents time decay. It measures how much value an option loses each day as it approaches its expiration date. Theta is always a negative number for option buyers because time is working against them. The closer an option gets to expiration, the faster its Theta (and time decay) typically increases.

**Vega** measures an option's sensitivity to changes in implied volatility. If the market expects the stock to become more volatile, Vega tells you how much the option's price will increase. Even if the stock price doesn't move, a rise in volatility can increase an option's value, while a drop in volatility can decrease it.

By monitoring the Greeks, traders can gain a deeper understanding of why their options are changing in value and make more informed decisions about when to enter or exit a trade.
