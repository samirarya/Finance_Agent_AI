# The Role of International Trade

No country can produce everything its citizens want at the lowest possible cost. This is why international trade is so important. It allows countries to focus on what they are best at—a concept economists call "comparative advantage."

When countries trade, they can access goods and services that would be too expensive or impossible to produce at home. For example, the U.S. might export high-tech software and aircraft while importing coffee from Brazil and electronics from Taiwan. This specialization leads to greater efficiency and lower prices for consumers globally.

International trade is measured by the "Balance of Trade." If a country sells more than it buys, it has a "trade surplus." If it buys more than it sells, it has a "trade deficit."

Trade can be a sensitive topic because while it benefits consumers through lower prices, it can also lead to job losses in specific domestic industries that can't compete with cheaper foreign labor. This often leads to "protectionism"—the use of tariffs (taxes on imports) or quotas to limit trade. While protectionism might save specific jobs in the short term, it usually leads to higher prices and slower economic growth for everyone in the long run. In our modern, globalized world, understanding trade is essential for understanding why your phone was designed in one country and built in another.
