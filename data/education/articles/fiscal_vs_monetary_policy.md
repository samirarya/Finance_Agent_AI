# Fiscal vs. Monetary Policy

When the economy needs a boost or needs to be slowed down, the government has two main sets of tools: fiscal policy and monetary policy. While they have similar goals, they are managed by different people and work in different ways.

Fiscal Policy is managed by the President and Congress. It involves changing government spending and tax rates. If the economy is slow, the government might "stimulate" it by spending more on infrastructure or by cutting taxes to put more money in people's pockets. Conversely, if the economy is overheating, they might cut spending or raise taxes to reduce the national debt and slow down growth.

Monetary Policy is managed by the central bank (the Federal Reserve in the U.S.). It involves changing the money supply and interest rates. As we've seen, the Fed can lower rates to encourage borrowing and spending, or raise rates to fight inflation.

In a perfect world, fiscal and monetary policy work together like the accelerator and the brake on a car. However, because fiscal policy is political and takes a long time to pass, monetary policy is often the first line of defense during an economic crisis. Understanding these two levers helps you see how the "rules of the game" are changing and how those changes will affect your investments and your wallet.
