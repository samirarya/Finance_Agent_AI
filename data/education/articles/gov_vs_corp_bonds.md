# Government vs. Corporate Bonds

Bonds are primarily issued by two types of entities: governments and corporations. While both involve lending money in exchange for interest, they carry different levels of risk and reward.

Government bonds are issued by national, state, or local governments. U.S. Treasury bonds are backed by the "full faith and credit" of the U.S. government and are considered among the safest investments in the world. Because the risk of default is extremely low, they typically offer lower interest rates. Investors use them as a "risk-free" benchmark and a safe haven during economic uncertainty.

Corporate bonds are issued by companies to fund operations, expansion, or acquisitions. These are generally riskier than government bonds because companies can go bankrupt. To compensate for this higher risk, corporate bonds usually offer higher interest rates. The level of risk varies greatly depending on the financial health of the company; a bond from a stable utility company will have a much lower interest rate than one from a struggling startup.

Choosing between government and corporate bonds depends on your risk tolerance and income needs. Conservative investors often favor the safety of treasuries, while those looking for higher yields may venture into the corporate bond market. Diversifying across both types can help balance a fixed-income portfolio.
