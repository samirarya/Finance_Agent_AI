# The Importance of the Expense Ratio

If you only look at one number when comparing mutual funds, make it the "expense ratio." This represents the total annual fee that the fund charges to manage your investment, expressed as a percentage of your total assets in the fund.

For example, if a fund has an expense ratio of 1.00%, you will pay $100 per year for every $10,000 you have invested. This fee is automatically deducted from the fund's assets, so you don't receive a bill, but it directly reduces your return.

Expense ratios cover the salaries of the fund managers, the cost of research, marketing (12b-1 fees), and administrative expenses. While it might seem like a small percentage, the impact over many years is massive. Because of compounding, every dollar paid in fees is a dollar that isn't growing in your account. Over 30 years, a difference between a 0.10% fee and a 1.00% fee can cost you nearly 20% of your total potential nest egg.

In general, look for funds with "low" expense ratios. For index funds, this usually means 0.15% or less. For actively managed funds, 0.50% to 0.75% is considered reasonable, while anything over 1.00% is often viewed as expensive. In the world of investing, you don't necessarily get what you pay for—in fact, lower-cost funds often perform better than higher-cost ones simply because they have a lower hurdle to clear.
