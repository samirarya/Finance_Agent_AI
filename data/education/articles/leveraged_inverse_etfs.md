# Leveraged and Inverse ETFs: High Risk, High Reward

Most ETFs are designed to track their underlying index on a 1-to-1 basis. However, there is a specialized class of funds known as leveraged and inverse ETFs that are designed for more aggressive, short-term strategies.

Leveraged ETFs use financial derivatives to amplify the returns of an index. For example, a "2x" leveraged ETF aims to deliver double the daily return of its target. If the index goes up 1%, the ETF goes up 2%. The catch, of course, is that if the index goes down 1%, the ETF loses 2%.

Inverse ETFs, also called "short" ETFs, are designed to profit when an index falls. If the index drops 1%, the inverse ETF gains 1%. There are even leveraged inverse ETFs that provide amplified gains when the market declines.

It is crucial to understand that these funds are meant for daily trading, not long-term investing. Because of a process called "volatility decay," their performance over weeks or months can diverge significantly from the target index. They are high-risk instruments that can lead to rapid, substantial losses if the market moves against you. For most beginner and intermediate investors, these funds are best avoided in favor of traditional, long-term oriented ETFs.
