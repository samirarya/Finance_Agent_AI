# ETFs vs. Index Mutual Funds: Which is Better?

While ETFs and index mutual funds share many similarities—both provide diversification and often track an index—they have key differences that might make one a better fit for your specific situation.

The main difference is how they are traded. ETFs are traded on an exchange throughout the day, just like stocks. You can buy or sell them at any time at the current market price. Mutual funds, however, are only priced once a day after the market closes. All orders placed during the day are executed at that single closing price.

Another difference is the cost. Both can have very low fees, but ETFs often have a slight edge. Additionally, some mutual funds require a minimum initial investment (e.g., $3,000), whereas you can buy as little as one share of an ETF. However, if your brokerage charges commissions for trading, the cost of frequent ETF trades could add up, while many mutual funds can be bought without a commission.

Tax efficiency is another factor. Due to their unique structure, ETFs generally generate fewer capital gains distributions than mutual funds, which can result in a lower tax bill for investors holding them in taxable accounts. Ultimately, for long-term investors, both are excellent tools. ETFs offer more flexibility and potentially lower taxes, while mutual funds can be easier for automatic monthly investing.
