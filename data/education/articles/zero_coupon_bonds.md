# Zero-Coupon Bonds: Buying at a Discount

Most bonds pay interest twice a year, but "zero-coupon" bonds work differently. As the name suggests, they pay no periodic interest (no "coupons"). Instead, these bonds are sold at a deep discount to their face value.

The return for the investor comes from the difference between the discounted purchase price and the full face value received when the bond matures. For example, you might buy a $1,000 face-value bond for $600 today. If it matures in 10 years, your "interest" is the $400 gain you realize at the end of that decade.

Zero-coupon bonds are popular for investors who have a specific financial goal on a fixed date, such as a child's college tuition or a planned retirement date. You know exactly how much you will receive and when.

One unique aspect of zero-coupon bonds is their tax treatment. Even though you don't receive cash interest each year, the IRS requires you to pay taxes on the "phantom income"—the portion of the discount that is considered earned each year. For this reason, many investors prefer to hold zero-coupon bonds in tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs. They are also more sensitive to interest rate changes than regular bonds, so be prepared for more price volatility if you sell before maturity.
