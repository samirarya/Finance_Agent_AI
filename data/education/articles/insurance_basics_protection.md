# Insurance Basics: Life, Health, and Disability

Most of financial planning is about building assets, but just as important is protecting those assets from disaster. This is where insurance comes in. It is a way to transfer the risk of a catastrophic event from yourself to an insurance company.

Health Insurance: This is non-negotiable. A single major surgery or illness can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars and lead to bankruptcy. Even a "catastrophic" high-deductible plan is better than no coverage at all.

Life Insurance: If someone else depends on your income (like a spouse or children), you need life insurance. "Term Life" is generally the best choice for most people; it's inexpensive and provides a large payout if you die during your peak earning years. Avoid "Whole Life" unless you have a very complex estate planning need, as it is much more expensive and a poor investment.

Disability Insurance: Your most valuable asset isn't your house or your car—it's your ability to earn an income. If you become injured and can't work for five years, how would you pay your bills? Disability insurance provides a percentage of your salary if you are unable to work. Many employers offer this as a benefit, but you should check if the coverage is sufficient.

Insurance is the foundation of a solid financial house. You hope you never need it, but having it ensures that a single accident doesn't wipe out a lifetime of hard work.
