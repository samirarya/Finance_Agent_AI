# Gross Domestic Product (GDP) Explained

Gross Domestic Product, or GDP, is the most common way we measure the size and health of a country's economy. It represents the total dollar value of all goods and services produced within a country's borders over a specific period, usually a year.

Think of GDP as the "scorecard" for the economy. It is made up of four main components:
1. Consumption: What regular people spend on things like food, clothes, and haircuts. This is the biggest part of the U.S. economy.
2. Investment: What businesses spend on things like new factories, equipment, and software.
3. Government Spending: What federal, state, and local governments spend on roads, schools, and the military.
4. Net Exports: The value of what a country sells to other countries minus what it buys from them.

When GDP is growing, the economy is healthy—businesses are hiring, and people are spending. This is known as "expansion." When GDP shrinks for two quarters in a row, the economy is officially in a "recession."

While GDP is a great overall measure, it doesn't tell the whole story. It doesn't measure things like income inequality, environmental health, or the quality of life. However, for investors and policymakers, it remains the primary way to track whether the economy is getting bigger or smaller.
