# Common vs. Preferred Stock

When you hear people talk about "stocks," they are usually referring to common stock. However, there is another type of share known as preferred stock. While both represent ownership in a company, they come with different rights and benefits.

Common stock is the most prevalent form of equity. It offers the potential for significant capital appreciation if the company succeeds. Shareholders of common stock typically have voting rights, allowing them to participate in major corporate decisions, such as electing the board of directors. If the company pays dividends, common shareholders receive them, though these payments are not guaranteed and can fluctuate.

Preferred stock functions somewhat like a hybrid between a stock and a bond. Preferred shareholders usually do not have voting rights, but they have a higher claim on the company's assets and earnings. This means if a company goes bankrupt, preferred shareholders are paid before common shareholders. Additionally, preferred stocks pay fixed dividends that must be paid before any dividends are distributed to common shareholders. This makes the income more predictable, similar to interest payments on a bond.

For most individual investors, common stock is the primary vehicle for building wealth over time. Preferred stock is often favored by investors who prioritize steady income and a degree of safety over high growth potential. Understanding these differences helps you choose the type of equity that best fits your investment strategy.
