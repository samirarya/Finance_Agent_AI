# Rebalancing Your Portfolio: Why and How

Once you've set your target asset allocation (for example, 70% stocks and 30% bonds), you can't just leave it forever. Over time, as different parts of the market perform differently, your portfolio will "drift" away from your target. This is where rebalancing comes in.

Imagine the stock market has a fantastic year, while bonds stay flat. Your 70/30 split might become 80/20. Now, you have more risk in your portfolio than you originally intended. If a market crash happens the next day, you'll lose more money than you were prepared for.

Rebalancing is the act of selling some of the assets that have done well and using that money to buy more of the assets that have underperformed. This brings you back to your target allocation.

While it feels counter-intuitive to "sell your winners" and "buy your losers," rebalancing is a disciplined way to follow the most important rule of investing: "Buy Low, Sell High." It forces you to take profits from overvalued areas and put them into undervalued ones. Most experts suggest rebalancing once a year, or whenever your allocation drifts by more than 5%. It is a vital maintenance step that keeps your portfolio's risk level consistent with your long-term goals.
