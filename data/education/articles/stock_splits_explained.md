# Stock Splits and Reverse Splits Explained

A stock split occurs when a company increases its number of outstanding shares to lower the price of each individual share. For example, in a 2-for-1 split, if you owned one share worth $100, you would now own two shares worth $50 each. The total value of your investment remains the same, but the stock is more accessible to a wider range of investors.

Companies often perform stock splits when their share price has risen so high that it might discourage smaller investors from buying. While a split doesn't change the company's underlying value, it can sometimes lead to increased demand as the stock becomes more affordable, which might push the price up slightly in the short term.

A reverse stock split is the opposite. The company reduces its number of shares to increase the price of each share. In a 1-for-5 reverse split, if you owned five shares worth $1 each, you would now own one share worth $5. Reverse splits are often seen as a sign of trouble. Companies might do this to avoid being delisted from an exchange that has a minimum share price requirement, or to make the stock look more attractive to institutional investors who avoid "penny stocks."

Understanding splits is important because they can change the look of your portfolio and the historical price charts. However, it's crucial to remember that a split itself doesn't create or destroy value—it's simply a change in how the ownership is divided.
