# Social Security: Understanding Your Benefits

Social Security is a federal program designed to provide a baseline of income for retirees, disabled individuals, and survivors. For most Americans, it is a critical piece of the retirement puzzle, but it was never intended to be the only source of income.

The amount you receive from Social Security is based on your highest 35 years of earnings. You pay into the system through payroll taxes throughout your working life.

One of the most important decisions you'll make is when to start taking your benefits. You can start as early as age 62, but your monthly payment will be permanently reduced. If you wait until your "Full Retirement Age" (usually 66 or 67), you'll get 100% of your benefit. If you delay even further—up until age 70—your benefit will increase by about 8% for every year you wait.

Deciding when to claim involves balancing your current health, your other retirement savings, and your life expectancy. If you are in good health and can afford to wait, delaying benefits often provides a much larger guaranteed, inflation-adjusted income stream for the rest of your life. You can check your estimated future benefits by creating an account on the Social Security Administration's website (ssa.gov).
