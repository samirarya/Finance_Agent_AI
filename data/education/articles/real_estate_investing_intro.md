# Introduction to Real Estate Investing

Real estate has long been a popular way for individuals to build wealth and generate income. Unlike stocks or bonds, real estate is a tangible asset—something you can see and touch. There are several ways to participate in this market, each with its own level of risk and involvement.

The most traditional method is owning physical property. This can be residential (like a single-family home or an apartment building) or commercial (like an office building or a retail space). As an owner, you can make money in two ways: through rental income and through appreciation (the increase in the property's value over time).

One of the unique advantages of real estate is "leverage." You can buy a property worth $500,000 with only $100,000 of your own money by taking out a mortgage. If the property value goes up by 10%, you've made $50,000 on your $100,000 investment—a 50% return. However, leverage is a double-edged sword; if the property value falls, your losses are also amplified.

Real estate also offers significant tax benefits, such as the ability to deduct mortgage interest, property taxes, and depreciation. For those who don't want the hassle of being a landlord, there are other options like REITs or crowdfunding. Regardless of the method, real estate provides diversification that can help stabilize a portfolio dominated by stocks.
