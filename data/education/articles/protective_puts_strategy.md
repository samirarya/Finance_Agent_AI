# The Protective Put: Insurance for Your Portfolio

A protective put is an options strategy used to guard against a decline in the value of a stock you already own. It is often referred to as "portfolio insurance" because it sets a "floor" or a minimum price at which you can sell your shares, no matter how low the market price drops.

To use this strategy, an investor buys one put option for every 100 shares of stock they own. The investor pays a premium for this right, which is the cost of the "insurance policy." If the stock price stays above the strike price of the put, the investor simply loses the premium but benefits from the stock's continued growth. However, if the stock price crashes, the put option gains value, offsetting the losses on the stock.

For example, imagine you own 100 shares of a stock trading at $100. You are worried about a market dip, so you buy a protective put with a strike price of $90 for a $2 premium. If the stock drops to $70, you can still exercise your put and sell your shares for $90. Your loss is limited to $10 per share (the difference between $100 and $90) plus the $2 premium, rather than the $30 loss you would have faced without the put.

The main drawback of the protective put is the cost. Just like car or home insurance, you pay a premium for protection you hope you never have to use. If the stock price remains stable or rises, the premium you paid for the put reduces your overall return. Despite the cost, many investors use protective puts during periods of high market volatility to give themselves peace of mind and protect their hard-earned capital.
