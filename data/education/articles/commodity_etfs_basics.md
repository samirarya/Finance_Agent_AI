# Commodity ETFs: Gold, Oil, and More

Commodities are physical goods like gold, silver, oil, natural gas, wheat, or corn. Historically, investing in commodities was difficult for individual investors, requiring specialized futures accounts. Commodity ETFs have changed that, making it easy to add these assets to a standard brokerage account.

There are two main ways commodity ETFs work. Some are "physically backed," meaning the fund actually owns and stores the physical asset. Gold ETFs are the most common example of this; for every share you buy, the fund holds a specific amount of physical gold in a secure vault. This provides a direct and safe way to track the price of the metal.

Other commodity ETFs use futures contracts—agreements to buy or sell the asset at a later date. These are more common for commodities that are difficult to store, like oil or agricultural products. These funds can be more complex and may not track the "spot" price of the commodity perfectly due to the costs of rolling those contracts over.

Commodities are often used as a hedge against inflation or to provide diversification, as their prices often move differently than stocks and bonds. However, they can be highly volatile and do not pay dividends or interest. For most investors, a small allocation to a broad commodity ETF or a gold ETF is sufficient.
