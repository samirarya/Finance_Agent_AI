# Risk Tolerance: Determining Your Investment Profile

Before you put a single dollar into the market, you must understand your "Risk Tolerance." This is your ability and willingness to lose some or all of your original investment in exchange for the possibility of greater returns.

Risk tolerance is made up of two parts:
1. Risk Capacity: This is the mathematical part. If you are 22 years old, you have a high capacity because you have 40 years to recover from a market crash. If you are 64, your capacity is low because you need that money soon.
2. Risk Attitude: This is the emotional part. How will you feel if you log into your account and see that your $100,000 is now worth $70,000? If you will lose sleep and sell everything in a panic, you have a low risk attitude.

There is no "right" risk tolerance. A person with high risk tolerance might have a portfolio of 100% stocks. Someone with a low tolerance might prefer 40% stocks and 60% bonds.

The key is to build a portfolio that you can "stick with." The worst thing an investor can do is choose a high-risk portfolio during a bull market and then sell everything at the bottom of a bear market. Be honest with yourself about how much volatility you can handle. A "perfect" portfolio on paper is useless if it causes you to make a panic-driven mistake in the real world.
