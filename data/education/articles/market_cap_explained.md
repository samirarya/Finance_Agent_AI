# Market Capitalization: Small, Mid, and Large Cap

Market capitalization, or "market cap," is a simple yet vital metric used to measure the total value of a publicly traded company. It is calculated by multiplying the current stock price by the total number of outstanding shares. Investors use market cap to categorize companies and understand their size, risk profile, and growth potential.

Large-cap companies generally have a market capitalization of $10 billion or more. These are often household names—mature, stable companies with a long history of success. They tend to be less volatile than smaller companies and frequently pay dividends. While they might not grow as rapidly as startups, they offer a level of safety and reliability for conservative investors.

Mid-cap companies typically fall between $2 billion and $10 billion in market cap. These businesses are often in a phase of significant growth or expansion. They may offer a balance between the stability of large-caps and the growth potential of small-caps. Mid-caps can be more volatile but also provide opportunities for higher returns as they work toward becoming industry leaders.

Small-cap companies have market caps between $300 million and $2 billion. These are often younger companies or those serving niche markets. Small-caps can be highly volatile and carry more risk, but they also offer the greatest potential for explosive growth. Understanding market cap helps investors diversify their portfolios across different company sizes to manage risk and capture various growth opportunities.
