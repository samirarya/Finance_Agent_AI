# The Role of Stock Exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ)

A stock exchange is a centralized platform where buyers and sellers come together to trade shares of publicly listed companies. These institutions play a vital role in the global economy by providing liquidity, transparency, and regulation to the financial markets.

The New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) is the oldest and largest exchange in the world by market capitalization. Historically known for its physical trading floor on Wall Street, it now operates as a hybrid model, combining electronic and human-mediated trading. The NYSE is home to many of the world's most established blue-chip companies.

The NASDAQ, on the other hand, was the first electronic stock exchange. It is famous for hosting many of the world's leading technology and growth companies. Unlike the NYSE, the NASDAQ has no physical trading floor; all transactions are handled through a computer network.

Exchanges ensure that trading happens in a fair and orderly manner. They set listing requirements that companies must meet to be traded, such as financial disclosures and minimum share prices. By providing a standardized environment, exchanges make it possible for individual investors to buy and sell stocks with confidence, knowing that prices are public and transactions are secure. Without stock exchanges, the process of investing would be far more difficult, risky, and expensive for the average person.
