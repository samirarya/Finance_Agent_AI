# Interest Rates and the Federal Reserve

The Federal Reserve, often called "the Fed," is the central bank of the United States. Its primary job is to keep the economy stable by managing inflation and employment. Its most powerful tool is the ability to influence interest rates.

When the Fed wants to slow down inflation, it raises interest rates. This makes it more expensive for people to borrow money for cars and houses, and more expensive for businesses to expand. This "tighter" money slows down spending, which helps cool off rising prices.

When the economy is struggling or in a recession, the Fed does the opposite: it lowers interest rates. This makes borrowing cheaper, which encourages people to spend and businesses to invest, hopefully "jump-starting" economic growth.

Interest rates are often called the "price of money." They affect everything in your financial life. When the Fed raises rates, your savings account might earn more interest, but your credit card and mortgage rates will also go up. For investors, rising interest rates are often bad for stock and bond prices in the short term. By following the Fed's decisions, you can get a sense of where the experts think the economy is heading and prepare your finances accordingly.
