# The Business Cycle: Expansion and Recession

The economy does not grow in a straight line. Instead, it moves in a series of ups and downs known as the business cycle. This cycle has four main phases:

1. Expansion: This is the "good times." GDP is growing, unemployment is falling, and consumer confidence is high. Stock prices usually rise during this phase.
2. Peak: This is the highest point of the cycle. The economy is "overheating," and inflation may start to become a problem.
3. Contraction (Recession): This is a period of decline. GDP shrinks, businesses slow down, and unemployment begins to rise. A recession is officially defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth.
4. Trough: This is the "bottom." The decline stops, and the economy begins to stabilize. This eventually leads back into a new expansion phase.

The business cycle is a natural part of a market economy. While recessions are painful, they also serve to "clear out" inefficiencies and set the stage for the next period of growth.

For investors, the key is to remember that the stock market usually looks ahead. Stock prices often start falling before a recession begins and start rising before the recession is over. Understanding where we are in the cycle can help you manage your emotions—knowing that expansions don't last forever, but neither do recessions.
