# Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) Explained

The IRS gives you tax breaks on Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s to encourage you to save for retirement. However, they don't want the money to stay there forever. They eventually want their tax cut. This is why "Required Minimum Distributions" (RMDs) exist.

An RMD is the minimum amount you must withdraw from your retirement accounts each year once you reach a certain age (currently 73, though it is scheduled to rise to 75 in the coming years). The amount you must withdraw is calculated by dividing your account balance at the end of the previous year by a life expectancy factor provided by the IRS.

If you fail to take your RMD, the penalty is severe—historically 50% of the amount you should have withdrawn, though recently reduced to 25% (or 10% if corrected quickly).

It's important to plan for RMDs because they can push you into a higher tax bracket and may increase the taxes you pay on Social Security. One way to avoid RMDs is by using a Roth IRA, which currently does not require distributions during the original owner's lifetime. If you don't need the money from your RMD for living expenses, you can reinvest it into a taxable brokerage account or use it for charitable giving through a "Qualified Charitable Distribution" (QCD) to avoid the tax hit.
