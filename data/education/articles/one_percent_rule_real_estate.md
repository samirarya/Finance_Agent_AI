# The 1% Rule in Rental Properties

When you are looking at dozens of potential rental properties, you need a quick way to filter out the "duds" from the "gems." This is where the 1% Rule comes in. It is a simple "rule of thumb" used by real estate investors to gauge whether a property's income is likely to cover its expenses.

The 1% Rule states that a property should rent for at least 1% of its total purchase price (including any immediate repairs). For example, if you buy a house for $200,000, it should bring in at least $2,000 per month in rent.

If a property meets the 1% rule, it has a high probability of producing positive cash flow. If it only rents for 0.5% ($1,000 on a $200k house), it's very likely that the mortgage, taxes, and maintenance will be more than the rent, meaning you'll have to pay money out of your own pocket every month to keep the house.

It's important to note that the 1% rule is harder to meet in expensive, high-growth markets like San Francisco or New York, where investors are often banking on appreciation rather than current income. However, in many parts of the country, it remains a vital "sanity check." Use the 1% rule to narrow your search, but always perform a deep dive into the actual numbers before making an offer.
