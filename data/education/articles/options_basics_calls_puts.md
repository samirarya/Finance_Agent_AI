# Options Basics: Understanding Calls and Puts

Options are financial instruments known as derivatives because their value is derived from an underlying asset, such as a stock. Unlike owning a stock outright, an option gives you the right—but not the obligation—to buy or sell that stock at a specific price within a set timeframe. There are two primary types of options: calls and puts.

A call option gives the holder the right to buy a stock. Investors typically buy calls when they believe the stock price will rise. If the stock price increases significantly, the call holder can purchase the stock at a lower price (the strike price) and potentially sell it for a profit. If the stock price doesn't rise, the investor only loses the amount paid for the option, known as the premium.

A put option gives the holder the right to sell a stock. Investors usually buy puts when they expect the stock price to fall. A put option acts like an insurance policy; if the stock price drops, the holder can still sell their shares at the higher strike price, protecting their investment or profiting from the decline.

Understanding calls and puts is the foundation of options trading. While they offer the potential for high returns with a smaller initial investment, they also come with unique risks, primarily the fact that they can expire worthless if the stock doesn't move as expected within the allotted time. Beginners should start by mastering these two basic building blocks before moving on to more complex strategies.
