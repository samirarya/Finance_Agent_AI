# Understanding Tax Brackets and Marginal Rates

One of the most common misunderstandings in finance is how income tax brackets work. Many people fear that if they get a raise that pushes them into a "higher bracket," they will actually take home less money. Fortunately, that's not how it works in the United States and many other countries. We use a "progressive" or "marginal" tax system.

Think of your income like a series of buckets. The first $11,000 you earn goes into the 10% bucket. You pay 10% on that money. The next amount you earn—up to about $44,000—goes into the 12% bucket. You only pay 12% on the money in that specific bucket, not on your total income.

Your "marginal tax rate" is the rate you pay on your very last dollar of income. Your "effective tax rate" is the actual percentage of your total income that goes to the government after all the different brackets are averaged out. Your effective rate will always be lower than your marginal rate.

Understanding this is crucial for making financial decisions. It means that a raise is always a good thing—you will always keep the majority of your new income. It also helps you understand the value of tax deductions; if you are in the 22% bracket, a $1,000 deduction saves you $220 in taxes.
