# Unemployment Rates and Their Impact

The unemployment rate is one of the most closely watched economic indicators. It represents the percentage of the labor force that is jobless and actively looking for work. It is a vital measure of an economy's health and a major factor in political and financial decisions.

There are three main types of unemployment:
1. Frictional: This is "natural" unemployment. It's people who are between jobs, such as new graduates or people who quit to find a better position.
2. Structural: This happens when there is a mismatch between the skills workers have and the skills employers need. For example, if a factory automates, workers may need to learn new tech skills to find a job.
3. Cyclical: This is the "bad" kind. It's caused by a downturn in the business cycle. When the economy slows down, businesses lay off workers because there isn't enough demand for their products.

When unemployment is low, the economy is usually strong. Consumers have money to spend, which fuels growth. However, if it's *too* low, it can lead to "wage-push inflation" as companies compete for a limited number of workers.

For the Fed, managing unemployment is half of its "dual mandate" (the other half is managing inflation). High unemployment is not just a statistic; it represents lost income, lower consumer spending, and significant social stress. Tracking these trends helps you understand the overall temperature of the economy.
