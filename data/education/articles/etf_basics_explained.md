# What is an ETF? A Simple Explanation

An Exchange-Traded Fund, or ETF, is a type of investment fund that is traded on a stock exchange, much like an individual stock. It allows investors to buy a basket of different assets—such as stocks, bonds, or commodities—in a single transaction.

Think of an ETF as a "container" for other investments. For example, an S&P 500 ETF holds shares in all 500 of the companies in that index. When you buy one share of the ETF, you are getting instant diversification across hundreds of businesses. This is much cheaper and easier than trying to buy shares in each of those 500 companies individually.

ETFs offer several advantages. First, they are cost-effective; they often have very low management fees (expense ratios) because many of them are passively managed, simply tracking an index. Second, they offer high transparency; you can see exactly what assets the ETF holds at any time. Finally, they provide flexibility; because they trade on an exchange, you can buy or sell them at any time during the trading day at the current market price.

For most investors, ETFs have become the preferred way to build a diversified portfolio. Whether you want to invest in the entire stock market, a specific industry like technology, or a physical asset like gold, there is likely an ETF that fits your needs.
