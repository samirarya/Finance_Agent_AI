# Options Pricing: Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Value

The price of an option, known as the premium, is composed of two distinct parts: intrinsic value and extrinsic value. Understanding the difference between the two is key to knowing whether an option is "expensive" or "cheap" and how its price might change over time.

**Intrinsic value** is the "real" or tangible value of an option based on the current stock price. It is the amount by which an option is "in-the-money." For a call option, intrinsic value is the stock price minus the strike price (if positive). For a put option, it is the strike price minus the stock price (if positive). If an option is "out-of-the-money," its intrinsic value is zero. For example, if a stock is at $110 and you own a $100 call, the intrinsic value is $10.

**Extrinsic value**, also called "time value," is the portion of the premium that is NOT intrinsic value. It represents the market's expectation that the option might become more valuable before it expires. Extrinsic value is influenced by several factors, including the time remaining until expiration, the volatility of the stock, and current interest rates.

As an option approaches its expiration date, its extrinsic value gradually shrinks to zero—a process known as time decay. On the day of expiration, an option's price will consist entirely of its intrinsic value. This is why "out-of-the-money" options, which have zero intrinsic value, will expire worthless if they don't move "into-the-money" before the deadline.

By breaking down an option's price into these two categories, traders can better understand what they are paying for. Are you paying for "real" value, or are you paying for "time" and the "hope" that the stock will move in your favor?
