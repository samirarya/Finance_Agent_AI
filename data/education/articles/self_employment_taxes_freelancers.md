# Self-Employment Taxes: A Guide for Freelancers

When you work for an employer, they pay half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes, and you pay the other half through payroll deductions. When you work for yourself as a freelancer or small business owner, you are both the employer and the employee—which means you have to pay both halves. This is known as the "Self-Employment Tax."

The total self-employment tax rate is currently 15.3%. This is in addition to your regular income tax. It can be a shock to new freelancers who are used to having taxes automatically taken out of their checks.

To manage this, self-employed individuals are usually required to make "Estimated Tax Payments" to the IRS four times a year. If you wait until April to pay everything, you might face underpayment penalties.

The good news is that you can deduct the "employer half" of the self-employment tax (7.65%) when calculating your adjusted gross income. You can also deduct legitimate business expenses—like your home office, equipment, and marketing costs—which lowers your taxable income.

The key to success as a freelancer is to set aside 25-30% of every check you receive into a separate savings account for taxes. By planning ahead and making your quarterly payments, you can avoid a massive tax bill and focus on growing your business.
