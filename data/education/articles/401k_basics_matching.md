# 401(k) Basics: The Power of Employer Matching

A 401(k) is a retirement savings plan sponsored by an employer. It allows employees to save and invest a portion of their paycheck before taxes are taken out. This "pre-tax" status means that contributing to a 401(k) actually lowers your current tax bill.

The most powerful feature of many 401(k) plans is the "employer match." Many companies will match a portion of your contributions—for example, "50 cents for every dollar you contribute, up to 6% of your salary." This is essentially a 50% instant return on your money. It is the closest thing to "free money" in the financial world. If your employer offers a match and you aren't contributing enough to get the full amount, you are leaving money on the table.

Inside a 401(k), you typically choose from a menu of investment options, usually a selection of mutual funds or target-date funds. The money grows tax-deferred, meaning you don't pay taxes on the growth until you withdraw the money in retirement.

There are limits to how much you can contribute each year, which the IRS adjusts periodically. Also, be aware of "vesting" schedules—the rules that determine when you actually own the money your employer has contributed. If you leave your job before you are "fully vested," you might lose some or all of the employer's match. For most workers, the 401(k) is the single most important tool for building retirement wealth.
