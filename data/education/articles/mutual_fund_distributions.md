# Mutual Fund Distributions: Dividends and Capital Gains

When you own a mutual fund, you can make money in two ways: through the increase in the share price (NAV) and through "distributions." Understanding how these distributions work is important, especially when it comes to taxes.

Throughout the year, the companies held by the mutual fund may pay dividends, or the fund's bonds may pay interest. Additionally, the fund manager may sell some stocks at a profit. By law, mutual funds are required to pass these earnings directly to their shareholders, usually once or twice a year.

These payments are called "distributions." There are two main types: "Dividend Distributions" (the income from dividends and interest) and "Capital Gains Distributions" (the profits from selling securities within the fund).

Most investors choose to "reinvest" these distributions. Instead of receiving a check, the money is used to buy more shares of the fund, which helps your investment grow through compounding. However, it's important to remember that in a taxable account, you generally have to pay taxes on these distributions in the year they are paid, even if you reinvest them. This is one reason why holding "tax-efficient" funds or using tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs is a common strategy for long-term investors.
