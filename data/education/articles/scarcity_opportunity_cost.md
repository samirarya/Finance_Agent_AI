# Scarcity and Opportunity Cost: Basic Concepts

If there's one thing that defines the entire study of economics, it's the concept of "Scarcity." Scarcity means that there are limited resources—time, money, labor, raw materials—but unlimited wants. Because we can't have everything, we must make choices.

Every choice we make has a cost. Not just the dollar cost, but the "Opportunity Cost." The opportunity cost is the value of the next best thing you *didn't* choose.

For example, if you spend $1,000 on a new TV, the opportunity cost isn't just the $1,000. It's also what else you could have done with that $1,000—perhaps putting it in a retirement account where it could have grown to $5,000 over ten years. If you spend an hour watching Netflix, the opportunity cost is what else you could have done with that hour, like exercising or learning a new skill.

In the world of investing, opportunity cost is everywhere. If you keep all your money in a savings account earning 0.1%, the "cost" is the 7% or 8% you could have earned in the stock market. By training yourself to think in terms of opportunity cost, you become a better decision-maker. You start to see that every "Yes" to one thing is a "No" to something else. This perspective is the foundation of a disciplined and successful financial life.
