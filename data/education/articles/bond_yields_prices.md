# Understanding Bond Yields and Prices

The relationship between bond prices and bond yields is one of the most fundamental concepts in finance. They share an inverse relationship: when bond prices go up, yields go down, and when bond prices go down, yields go up.

A bond's "coupon rate" is the fixed interest it pays based on its face value. However, once a bond is issued, it can be bought and sold on the secondary market, where its price fluctuates. The "yield" is the actual return an investor gets based on the price they paid for the bond.

For example, if you buy a $1,000 bond that pays $50 in annual interest, its yield is 5%. If interest rates in the economy rise to 6%, new bonds will be issued at that higher rate. To compete, your 5% bond must drop in price so that its $50 interest payment represents a 6% return for a new buyer. If you sell it then, you'll get less than $1,000.

Conversely, if interest rates fall, your bond with a higher interest rate becomes more valuable, and its price will rise. This inverse dance is why bond investors watch interest rate movements so closely. Understanding this relationship helps you manage the "interest rate risk" in your portfolio and interpret how changes in the economy will affect your bond holdings.
