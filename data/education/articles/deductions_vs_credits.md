# Deductions vs. Credits: Maximizing Your Refund

When you file your taxes, you want to lower your tax bill as much as possible. Two primary tools help you do this: tax deductions and tax credits. While they both save you money, they work in very different ways.

A tax deduction lowers the amount of income you are taxed on. If you earned $50,000 and have a $1,000 deduction, the IRS only taxes you as if you earned $49,000. The actual value of the deduction depends on your tax bracket. If you are in the 12% bracket, a $1,000 deduction saves you $120. Common deductions include student loan interest, 401(k) contributions, and charitable donations.

A tax credit is even more powerful. It is a dollar-for-dollar reduction of your actual tax bill. If you owe $3,000 in taxes and have a $1,000 tax credit, you now only owe $2,000. It doesn't matter what your tax bracket is—the credit is worth exactly $1,000 to everyone. Common credits include the Child Tax Credit and the Earned Income Tax Credit.

Some credits are "refundable," meaning that if the credit reduces your tax bill below zero, the government will actually send you a check for the difference. Deductions, on the other hand, can never reduce your tax bill below zero. In general, a credit is always more valuable than a deduction of the same amount.
