# Flipping Houses: Potential and Pitfalls

"Fix and flip" has become incredibly popular thanks to reality television. The concept is simple: you buy a distressed property at a low price, renovate it quickly, and sell it for a profit. While it sounds exciting, it is a high-risk, high-intensity strategy that is very different from long-term real estate investing.

The secret to a successful flip is the "Buy." Experienced flippers follow the "70% Rule": you shouldn't pay more than 70% of the property's "After Repair Value" (ARV) minus the cost of renovations. If a house will be worth $300,000 after $50,000 in repairs, you shouldn't pay more than $160,000 ($300k * 0.70 - $50k).

The pitfalls are numerous. Renovations almost always take longer and cost more than expected. Holding costs—such as interest on loans, property taxes, and insurance—eat into your profits every day the house isn't sold. Furthermore, a sudden cooling of the housing market can leave you stuck with a property you can't sell for a profit.

Flipping is not an investment; it is a job. It requires a deep understanding of the local market, a reliable team of contractors, and enough capital to handle surprises. For those with the right skills, it can generate significant wealth, but for the unprepared, it can lead to a financial nightmare.
