# Bonds 101: How Debt Securities Work

In simple terms, a bond is a loan made by an investor to a borrower, such as a corporation or a government. When you buy a bond, you are essentially acting as the bank. In exchange for your money, the borrower promises to pay you back the full amount (the principal) on a specific date in the future (the maturity date), and to pay you interest along the way.

The interest payments on a bond are often called "coupons." Most bonds pay a fixed interest rate, meaning you know exactly how much income you will receive and when you will receive it. This predictability is why bonds are frequently referred to as "fixed-income" investments.

Bonds are generally considered safer than stocks, as they have a higher priority for payment if a company faces financial trouble. However, they typically offer lower potential returns. The main risks associated with bonds are interest rate risk (prices go down when rates go up) and credit risk (the borrower might fail to pay).

For most investors, bonds serve as a stabilizer in a portfolio. While stocks provide growth, bonds provide steady income and a cushion during market downturns. Understanding the relationship between bond prices and interest rates is key: when interest rates rise, the value of existing bonds with lower rates typically falls, and vice versa.
