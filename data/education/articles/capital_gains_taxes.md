# Capital Gains Taxes: Short-Term vs. Long-Term

When you sell an investment for more than you paid for it, that profit is called a "capital gain." The IRS treats this profit as income, but the rate you pay depends on how long you held the investment before selling.

Short-Term Capital Gains apply to investments held for one year or less. These profits are taxed as "ordinary income," meaning they are added to your regular salary and taxed at your normal marginal rate (which can be as high as 37%).

Long-Term Capital Gains apply to investments held for more than one year. These are taxed at special, lower rates: 0%, 15%, or 20%, depending on your total income. For most middle-income investors, the rate is 15%. This is a significant discount compared to the regular income tax rates.

The takeaway for investors is clear: whenever possible, try to hold your investments for at least a year and a day. By doing so, you can significantly reduce the "tax drag" on your portfolio and keep more of your hard-earned profits. This is one of the many reasons why "buy and hold" is such a successful long-term strategy—not only does it give your money more time to grow, but it also ensures you pay the lowest possible tax rate when you finally decide to sell.
