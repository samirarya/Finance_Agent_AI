# Target-Date Funds: Investing for Retirement

For many people, the most stressful part of investing is deciding how to balance their money between stocks and bonds, and how to change that balance as they get older. Target-Date Funds (TDFs) are designed to solve this problem automatically.

A target-date fund is a "fund of funds" that is named after the year you expect to retire (e.g., "Target Retirement 2055"). When you are young and the retirement date is far away, the fund is aggressive, holding mostly stocks for maximum growth. As you get closer to your target date, the fund automatically and gradually shifts its allocation toward more conservative investments like bonds and cash. This is known as the "glide path."

The primary advantage of a TDF is that it's a "set it and forget it" solution. You don't need to worry about rebalancing your portfolio or deciding when to become more conservative; the fund's professionals do it for you. This makes them incredibly popular in workplace 401(k) plans.

However, keep an eye on the fees, as some TDFs can be more expensive than building the same portfolio yourself using simple index funds. Also, remember that a TDF is a "one-size-fits-all" solution; it doesn't know your specific risk tolerance or other assets. For many beginner to intermediate investors, however, the convenience and disciplined strategy of a TDF are well worth it.
